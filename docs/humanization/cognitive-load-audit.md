# Cognitive Load Audit

## Purpose

This audit identifies where EMS may feel too dense, too technical, or too intimidating for users.

The goal is not to remove capability. The goal is to present capability in a safer, clearer order.

## Overloaded Dashboard Risks

### Executive Analytics

Risk: too many strategic signals can make the page feel like a control room instead of a decision aid.

Simplification:
- lead with one top summary
- hide deeper charts behind drill-downs
- keep trend, risk, and next action visible first

### Governance Cockpit

Risk: approvals, blockers, and audit signals can blur together.

Simplification:
- separate blockers from approvals
- group items by owner and urgency
- use clear labels for policy versus workflow issues

### Workload Duty Analytics

Risk: fairness, distribution, and duty totals can be read as performance judgments.

Simplification:
- explain fairness first
- separate load concentration from individual evaluation
- show what should happen next, not just the statistics

### Optimization Dashboard

Risk: the user may confuse candidate plans, trace evidence, and final publication state.

Simplification:
- make candidate vs final state obvious
- surface only the most important constraint failures first
- explain why a plan is blocked in plain language

### Predictive and Futures Intelligence

Risk: users may read forecasts as facts and become overconfident or alarmed.

Simplification:
- show confidence clearly
- label forecasts as scenarios or predictions
- connect each result to the current operational context

## Confusing Table Risks

- Large comparison tables can hide the most important row
- Tables with too many status columns can feel like paperwork instead of guidance
- Tables should be accompanied by a one-line summary of what to notice first

## Intimidating Workflow Risks

- Multiple approval steps can make the process feel punitive
- Hidden state changes can make users fear breaking the system
- Deep technical names can discourage non-technical users

Simplification:
- name the next step in plain language
- explain why the step exists
- show what happens after the step is completed

## Governance Overload Risks

- Too many approval terms can confuse users
- Audit and approval can look like the same thing
- Publication controls can feel like barriers instead of safeguards

Simplification:
- explain governance as protection and traceability
- separate approval, audit, and publication
- keep the role of each gate visible

## Operational Overload Risks

- Staff may need to watch too many pages at once
- Alerts from different pages may arrive without a clear priority order
- Room, schedule, and distribution tasks can feel fragmented

Simplification:
- group related tasks into a single operational path
- prioritize urgent items clearly
- show the normal sequence for the day

## Alert Fatigue Risks

- Repeated warnings can start to feel ignorable
- Users may stop reading alerts if they are not actionable

Simplification:
- mark repeated warnings as patterns
- distinguish informational notices from blocking issues
- explain what action the alert requires

## Executive Overload Risks

- Executives may see too much detail and too little decision support
- High-level users need fewer metrics and more interpretation

Simplification:
- summarize the institution in a small number of signals
- add context only when the leader drills down
- keep the recommendation visible

## Recommended Humanization Changes

### Progressive Disclosure

Show summary first, then detail only when the user asks for it.

### Beginner Mode

Offer a simplified landing view with guided next steps and reduced chart density.

### Simplified Executive Mode

Show readiness, risk, workload pressure, and governance status only.

### Dashboard Prioritization

Prioritize the highest-risk operational views at the top of the navigation and documentation.

### Operational Summarization

Every dashboard should have a one-line explanation of what it means and what to do next.

## Audit Conclusion

EMS should feel calmer if the documentation always answers:

- what this page is for
- what matters most right now
- what action is needed
- when to escalate

If a page cannot answer those four questions, it needs simplification.
